Message from Principal Br Solomon Morris

Dear Golden Jubilarians, (Batch of 1975)

It is with profound pride and deep affection that I extend my heartfelt greetings to all our esteemed past pupils as you return to your alma mater to celebrate the Golden Jubilee of your graduating year. Your presence today, is a living testimony to the enduring spirit of this institution and the legacy it continues to uphold.

Clad in the symbolic colours of gold, maroon, and green, our school has always stood for excellence, courage, and growth. These colours are not merely worn; they are lived—through character, conviction, and service. Guided by our timeless motto, Facta Non Verba—Deeds, Not Words, generations of students have been inspired to translate values into action, ideals into service, and vision into leadership.

As our school anthem so powerfully reminds us, this is a place that nurtures "leaders in every field." Over the past five decades, each of you has carried forward the foundations laid within these walls—foundations built on discipline, integrity, faith, and purpose. "Here's where our lives are made; here's where our foundations are laid," and indeed, it is here that you first learned to plan your lives with a sense of responsibility for God and country.

Your journeys since leaving school have enriched the world in diverse and meaningful ways, and your achievements continue to inspire present and future generations. Yet, no matter how far life has taken you, the values imbibed here remain your compass—quietly guiding your steps and shaping your choices.

As you celebrate this remarkable milestone, may this reunion rekindle cherished memories, renew old friendships, and reaffirm the timeless bond you share with your school. May this reunion also help you to remember all the brothers who taught and guided you, shaped and moulded you into what you are today. On behalf of the management, staff, and students, I thank you for the legacy you have created and for the honour you bring to this institution.

May the years ahead be filled with good health, continued purpose, and the satisfaction of lives well lived—in the true spirit of Facta Non Verba.

With warm regards and best wishes,

Br. Solomon Morris

St. Edmund's School, Shillong, and the March of Time

Eminent journalist, writer, academic, and policy commentator, Sanjoy Hazarika (1969 Batch) wrote vividly about the physical transformation of his alma mater in Strangers in the Mist (first published in 1995):

"The buildings were of sturdy wood, and had a rough-hewn attractiveness about them. The best classroom was the Senior Cambridge room, located above a stone porch and looking out over the front lawn. These days, the buildings are of ugly concrete; the senior classroom has been pulled down, the solitary date palm tree has gone too…."

He went on to observe:

"…..Now the narrow road leading to the school is packed with Marutis, jeeps and outsized Contessa cars as school ends to the clanging of the great bell, located above the water tower in the old wing. The bell has remained constant in the midst of these frantic changes."

The nostalgia among past pupils for the wooden buildings and the slower pace of life of an earlier St Edmund's School is understandable. That era belonged to a time when the School functioned largely as a boarding institution, serving a smaller and more select student body. As educational priorities evolved, the School adapted to serve a wider local community — reflecting a shift from education as privilege to education as service.

The photographs that follow trace this steady passage of time through the School's landmarks, recording what has endured, what has changed, and what has quietly passed into memory.

The School Driveway – Then (1972) & Now

The solitary date palm tree

Contrary to popular belief, the solitary date palm survived the construction of the new School building, but has since disappeared.

The School bell - from water tower to Centenary Memorial Garden

The School bell, originally housed in the water-tank tower, was later relocated to the rooftop of a new concrete building before finally being enshrined in the Centenary Memorial Garden beneath the Blessed Edmund Rice façade in March 2025.

THE SCHOOL BUILDING

School Building 1954-1973

School Building 1954-1973

After the 1950 Assam Earthquake, many colonial-era buildings in Shillong were structurally reinforced. The replacement of the original wooden porch with heavy, load-bearing reinforced-concrete pillars reflects a mid-twentieth-century shift toward permanence and improved seismic stability.

Pre-1954 School Building - Original Assam-type architecture (1916)

Pre-1954 School Building

Completed in October 1916, the original heritage building of St. Edmund's School was among the finest examples of Assam-type architecture. Developed after the Great Assam Earthquake of 1897, this style was adopted widely across North East India, combining lightweight materials such as ikra reeds with a flexible wooden frame for seismic resilience.

School Building in 1975 - New reinforced concrete structure

School Building in 1975

By the early 1970s, the aging structure had become costly to maintain, while changing educational priorities called for increased access for local students and additional classroom space. With the encouragement and financial support of the Meghalaya Government, the old façade was dismantled and replaced by a reinforced concrete building aligned with the School's expanded academic role. Construction began in December 1973 and classes commenced in 1975.

Summer House – Then (1975) and Now

School First Field – Then (1950s & 1960) & Now (2025)

School First Field – Then (1950s & 1960) & Now (2025)

In contrast to our time—when the School had four playing fields, including the F-Division or "Dhobi Field"—today only the First Field remains under the School's use. The other fields have since been allocated to Providence School, St Edmund's Higher Secondary School, and two NGO-run special schools, Mary Rice Centre and Jyoti Sroat School, all now located within the School campus. The First Field was upgraded with artificial turf in 2024.

Swimming Pool – Then & Now

Swimming Pool – Then & Now

The old swimming pool, enclosed by perforated steel panels made from US military surplus Marston Mats—originally used in wartime airfield construction—was a legacy of the School's military takeover during the Second World War. Its replacement by a modern indoor swimming complex at the far end of the First Field in 2022 marks a broader institutional shift toward facilities designed for safety, organised coaching, and year-round use.

The Pavilion (Then & Now)

The Pavilion (Then & Now)

It was used as a storeroom for sports equipment—cricket mats, nets, stumps, bats, pads and related gear. According to old school folklore, it served as a morgue during the Second World War, when the campus was requisitioned as a military hospital. The structure no longer stands, as evident in the present-day view.

The Chapel - Built in 1927

The Chapel

The Chapel was built in 1927 under the supervision of the then Principal, Br J. E. McCann. It was among the first ferro-concrete (reinforced concrete) structures to be constructed in Shillong, setting it apart from the earlier wooden buildings on the School campus. Despite its concrete shell, the interior was finished in teak wood and distinguished by fine, intricate carpentry. Carefully maintained over the decades, the Chapel stands preserved to this day as an iconic landmark of the campus.

The 110-year-old School Hall - Heritage structure

The School Hall

The 110-year-old School Hall — once also used as a study hall for boarders and now declared a heritage structure by the Meghalaya Heritage Authority (MHA) — survives to this day. Designed with excellent acoustics, it remains a hallmark of Assam-type construction.

The Stone Building - Constructed 1942 by U.S. Army

The Stone Building

Constructed in 1942 by the U.S. Army during the Second World War, it formed part of the military takeover of St Edmund's School, when the campus served as a hospital for Allied forces in the China–Burma–India theatre. Originally an operation theatre and X-ray block, it later housed a music / art & craft class, NCC offices, the Stationery Store, and the Tuck Shop. Today, it functions as a storeroom, its wartime utilitarian character still clearly evident.

Handball Court – Then & Now (Inaugurated 1972)

Handball Court – Then & Now

The handball court, inaugurated in 1972 by Br D.E. Hayes, has since been enclosed by walls and roofs and absorbed into later campus development. Its once open play surface now forms part of a building used for residential and institutional purposes. Although architecturally modest, its disappearance reflects the quiet, largely unrecorded transformations that have reshaped the School over the decades.

Three-storeyed Extension (1990s)

Boarding Section Extension

Following the closure of the boarding section in 1988, a three-storeyed extension was added in the 1990s to meet expanded academic needs. The old Senior Dormitory and Boarders' Refectory (Dining Hall) were dismantled to make way for this RCC block.

Providence School – Old & New Facilities (Established 1999)

Providence School

Providence School was established in 1999 by Br E.S. D'Souza to serve underprivileged children through education and life-skills training. Beginning as a small after-school programme, it initially functioned from existing St Edmund's facilities vacated after the closure of the boarding section in 1988. With steady growth in enrolment and scope, the School moved to its present premises on the site of the old E-Division (Junior) Field in 2023.

The Legend That Was Brother Eric Steven D'Souza

By Dr Pranjal Tahbildar

Brother Eric Steven D'Souza

The very first time that I laid eyes on him he was standing on the slope leading from the assembly area of the school to the Seniors' field, "Top Field" to all of us students of St. Edmund's School, Shillong. To me, a Class VII student then and waiting in the field with fellow students for instructions on the drill that we were to perform during the Annual Sports, I didn't know which struck me as his most prominent feature. Was it the mop of untidy curly black hair on a rather short and slight frame? Maybe it was the dark T-shirt matched with white trousers whose bottom (leg) parts flared out to over thirty (maybe even forty) inches? Or was it the bright red strips of cloth in the seams of the trousers below the knee which helped to convert the conservative white trousers into colourful, in-fashion, "bell-bots"? I took him to be an instructor who had been brought in from outside the school to teach us the drill that we were to perform. When a friend told me that he was one of the newer Brothers in school I laughed at him and said, "Don't be funny, maan"

I soon discovered that my friend was speaking the truth. The man in question was indeed from the Brotherhood, Rev. Br. Eric Steven D'Souza — Brother Daas to us during our school years — "Steve" to his many friends and fellow teachers and, in later years, simply "Dasu" to most of us. A flamboyant man who many of us thought would not last very long in the rather restricted confines of the Brotherhood, who would soon pack up his favourite tape-recorder, bell-bots, guitar and cigarette packets and leave, just as we had seen a few doing during our time in school. Yet leave he did not and, over five decades on, was still going as strongly as before, leaving his distinct and indelible mark in many a place and many a mind and many a heart. So much so that the former enigma became an icon, the former non-awe-inspiring bell-bottomed junior Brother became a legend.

I do not know much about Br D'Souza's background. My attempts to do so have met with failure, especially as he himself was averse to talking about himself or giving interviews. As far as my knowledge goes (and I would like to admit here that what I write today might have minor inaccuracies, and I would like to be excused for them, especially because my account is based on my memories of long ago and on the memories of other past students from Shillong who I have talked to) he was from a very well-to-do family settled in Bandra, Mumbai. His father, a veteran of the 1965 and 1971 Indo-Pak wars, retired from the Indian Army as a Major General. After clearing his Senior Cambridge from St. Mary's, Mt. Abu in 1965, the teenaged Steve decided that his calling lay in the service of education and humanity as part of the Irish Christian Brothers Congregation, and so got himself enrolled in the Brotherhood. He arrived in Shillong in the first half of the nineteen-seventies, and except for short stints in Kolkata and Delhi, served there for most of his career until illness and infirmity forced his relocation to Goa. Initially he was an excellent teacher of English, History and Geography in school, but I understand he later took up teaching Mathematics, Science and Computers too. In fact, one of India's first books on computers, "Chipping In" (1988), was authored by Dasu. Needless to say he taught these subjects equally well, instilling in the students not only knowledge, but a keen interest to learn more about the subject. With his innovative teaching techniques, the depth and breadth of his knowledge and his passion and unending energy for teaching, there was never a dull moment in his classroom. It would be inaccurate to say that I remember any particular class of his. In fact, I think I can remember all his classes! Flashes of recollection from different classes keep coming back to me, even after the elapse of so much time. Even his Moral Science classes evoke fond memories. I learnt so much in his English, History and Geography classes when he was our Class Teacher in Class IX that I can still manage a decent contribution in discussions with people much more qualified in these subjects. And I can still impress people who have limited knowledge about them! All this was thrown in with liberal doses of humour and child-like antics in his 'one-of-the-boys' demeanour. Class IX was, for me, probably the single best year of my school and college life. It is said that all good things come to an end and, unfortunately, sooner rather than later. Imagine all our dismay when we learnt that Brother D'Souza, who was to be our Class teacher in Class X also, was suddenly transferred to St Mary's Orphanage in Dum Dum. It was as if the sky had fallen on our heads. Many of us despaired that with him gone we could not expect to do very well in the ICSE Exams the following November.

That there is a benevolent God above who was concerned about our well-being soon became evident. Brother D'Souza asked us if we would be willing to attend some extra classes before and after regular school hours if he could arrange them. Needless to say we all said yes. Such was his dedication to teaching and his love for us that for the entire school year of 1975, till November when we appeared in our ICSE exams, he would travel all the way from Kolkata to Shillong, probably at his own expense, whenever he managed to get away from his work in Kolkata, and teach us in special classes. He did this because of his love for us and because he was worried about us, the first-ever batch of students to appear in the ICSE Examinations from North-east India, especially as there were no prescribed text books then. Our batch had a very high percentage of one-pointers in the three subjects that he taught us, and much fewer in the science subjects, even though we had very experienced teachers from St Edmund's College coming to teach us those. One day, at a social gathering in Dibrugarh, I overheard an ex-Loreto Convent (LC) girl speaking very highly of Dasu. On enquiry I learnt that he had extended his special classes for the Board Examinations to students of LC and Pine Mount (PM) School too, especially during times of crisis and curfews in Shillong when regular classes had to be suspended. He prodded and groomed all of us, boys and girls, into becoming better versions of ourselves.

At some point of time it seems that Brother D'Souza had started developing doubts about Society's need for Class X academic certificates. So, instead of an academic certificate, he wanted children to have, as he put it, "literacy, numeracy, financial literacy, and communication and media skills". He explained, "For example, I want a student from the confectionary classes to be able to make the confections, keep the accounts, communicate with potential customers in the local languages and in English, and carry on the trade". Towards this end he started "Providence", probably his greatest work. This is a school for children of financially weaker parents. Until recently it was housed in parts of the old St Edmund's School building. It is meant for boys and girls who would not otherwise be able to afford a decent education and would have been lost to the streets and dark lanes of the city. Here, in addition to instructions on the three "R's", students are taught several crafts and occupations so that they can fend for themselves in life. Things such as cooking, knitting, sewing, baking, confectionary-making, carpentry, masonry, candle-making and others are taught to all the children in the junior classes. Later, in the senior classes, depending on the interest and aptitude of the individual student, he or she is asked to concentrate on one or two crafts which could be useful later on in life. Another good aspect of this school is that senior students from St Edmund's, LC and PM were brought in to teach. This not only enriched the skill and experience of these more privileged students, but also sensitized them to the plight of the less-fortunate ones in society. So, even though Br D'Souza was not 'promoted' to Class X along with our batch at the end of 1974, he had truly excelled later on and 'promoted' himself from simple teaching in the classroom to teaching in the society at large. A massive testament to his compassionate heart and relentless drive to make a difference. Surely the crowning glory of a long and distinguished career?

When asked in an interview if he had looked to any real-life coach for inspiration in his portrayal of a hockey coach in Chak De India, superstar Shah Rukh Khan had said, "When I was studying at St Columba's in New Delhi, I had a teacher, Brother Eric D'Souza, who used to teach us soccer, hockey, cricket and various subjects apart from sport. He would be more an ideal teacher than a coach and has been instrumental in turning me into the person that I am". My feelings, exactly, for if there is one person who has influenced me more than anybody else, it is Brother D'Souza. The way he handled a teenage transgression on my part is something I not only remember, but has influenced my handling of similar incidents that I have encountered during my long years as a teacher and an administrator. His throwing the fear of God into me, and then forgiving me, nipped in the bud what could have developed into a dangerous flaw in my character. And with his students scattered all over the world, carrying on good work with the influence of at least part of his ideals and thoughts rubbed off on them, his is surely a life fulfilled. As a very highly-placed administrator in the Govt. of Meghalaya, impressed when she learnt that I was a past student of Br D'Souza's, told me, "You were taught by Br D'Souza? Wow! He is a living legend". Legend indeed. One that I was privileged and thankful to been influenced by. And proud and honoured to have known.

And now, he is no longer amongst us. On that fateful day of 13th October 2024, at Regina Mundi in Goa, where he had spent the final months of his life, he was taken away by the merciful hands of Death. It had been really very painful to witness one who had lived life so enthusiastically and so energetically bed-ridden by the crippling Progressive Supra-nuclear Palsy, having to depend on care-givers for every aspect of his existence. Tributes poured in from all over from his many students and admirers, from "my guiding light" by Shah Rukh Khan and many more, through having a "pivotal influence", "we are because you were" and being an "angel on earth" to the deifying "Bro Eric D'Souza has ended his visit to Planet Earth". In accordance with his wishes his funeral was held in his beloved Shillong. He was taken on one last journey to the city closest to his heart—a city to which he returned again and again, and to whose young citizens he devoted much of his selfless service, both within and beyond the call of duty. The funeral was attended by hundreds and witnessed live on the internet by thousands more across the globe. Again, as per his wish, "I did it my way" from Sinatra's "My way" was sung at his funeral service at St Edmund's School Chapel.

And Sir, Brother Daas, there is no doubt about that: unlike Marilyn Monroe, you were no candle in the wind. You always knew which way to go, and you always did it your way. Unlike her again, you remained steadfastly away from the limelight. You proved Shakespeare wrong too: the good that you have done has not been buried with your bones. It remains—in the minds, hearts, souls, and actions of the many whose lives you touched.

And though your candle has burnt out after a long, illuminating, incandescent existence, your legend lives on. It has indeed been an immense privilege to have been among your earliest students. Rest in peace Sir.

Eulogy for Bro Humphrey Brendan Dineen

By Mrinmoy Kanti Dasgupta & Roon Dowerah

Brother Humphrey Brendan Dineen

Bro Dineen came into our lives just as we entered our teenage years, designated as senior boys of the School.

Our years at school unfolded in three phases: Junior Boys, Middle Boys and Senior Boys. Aptly, our Class Teachers as Junior Boys (Class I to Class V) were female, and male Class Teachers were assigned from Class VI to Class VIII – and, all were Indian.

Bro Dineen's appointment as a Class Teacher (Section A) in our final two years in school proved to be a turning point for us, in many ways than one. Firstly, it was our first regular and direct classroom interaction with an Irish Brother, whose teaching methods and approach with the student fraternity was a paradigm shift from that of our hitherto Indian teachers in the lower classes (who were nevertheless, beloved and committed in their own right, appropriate for the age bracket of students). Secondly, the curriculum was more demanding in the final years in school, commensurate with the standards of Board Exams, and Bro Dineen delved into the subjects of English Literature, Moral Science and World History, which were his forte, with aplomb. Thirdly, he had that unmistakable Irish humour – wry, gentle and sharp, and that enlivened our otherwise serious subjects with amusing anecdotes and examples.

In our curriculum in English literature, we had Shakespeare's "Merchant of Venice" in Class IX, (which was a far cry from the more immediately comprehensible literature in Class VIII, like "Jacaranda Tree" by H.E. Bates and an abridged version of "The Three Musketeers "by Alexandre Dumas) which we perceived initially as a daunting task; however, Bro Dineen rendered this world renowned play, despite its intricate plot and archaic language, accessible and enjoyable for us boys—an experience many of us carried with us long after the final curtain. Of course, at the other end of the spectrum, we also had "Jungle Picture" by Norah Burke, which were simple narratives of man and beast in the Himalayan jungles: not one to be outdone, Bro Dineen narrated his own encounters with wildlife, like how, on a chilly dawn at Moradabad station, he recounted how he shot a tiger behind him with the help of a mirror over his shoulders! A tall tale indeed. More fantastical - how he shot two bears, with a single bullet. The bullet being shot at an impaled hatchet in the ground, the bullet being split in two and travelling accurately to the desired targets!!

We looked forward to his History classes when it came to World History from the chapters of "Years of Challenge" by CF Blount, which essentially focussed on the period of hundred years from late nineteenth century to about 1970. With his vast and deep knowledge of modern world history, his lectures were distinguished by exceptional clarity and successfully engaged our interest, thereby creating a lasting impression on our minds.

He was a towering personality, not only by physique, being a six footer, but by way of being a remarkable human being: a respected mentor, a person of wisdom and sound judgment, a father figure to many, forgiving, witty, and with an optimistic outlook on life. He would shuffle into the Classroom (yes, at age 62/63, rheumatoid arthritis was already setting in, notwithstanding the fact that he was an avid tennis player at that age!), and his commanding presence could be interpreted as intimidation ... that is, if you overlooked the mischievous twinkle in his eye!

One Latin phrase which he regularly recounted, especially during Moral Science Classes, was: Mens sana in corpore sano (meaning "a healthy mind in a healthy body". Along with the School motto "Facta non verba" (which again is Latin for "Deeds not Words", both these phrases have had an everlasting impact on our minds.

One cannot forget his classification of the students in our class in his inimitable wry Irish humour: the serious students (read front benchers) were "scholars" and the backbenchers were categorised as "students", with the "Philistines" being the incorrigible and precocious few at the bottom of the rung!

By the time he came to us in 1974, he had already spent a remarkable forty seven years in India. It would only be in the fitness of things that we render a brief account from his humble early years in Ireland, and then for the next sixty years from 1927, he dedicated his life to a land and people he loved -India:

He was one of seven children, born on the 8th of December 1912 in Rathmore on the Kerry-Cork border. His childhood and youth were governed and influenced by the farm in which he had an abiding interest. It is recorded that he gladly did his share of farmwork thus strengthening the virtues of hard work, fortitude and cheerfulness.

He arrived in Calcutta in 1927, when India was on a slow boil. There was talk of mutiny and revolt in the air. The British sun was setting, but the young Dineen was hardly aware of the rising nationalism that was about to influence this country in far reaching ways.

Br Dineen taught and ministered at St Joseph's Calcutta, The Calcutta Male Orphanage, St Joseph's Nainital, Goethals Kurseong, St Michael's Kurji (Patna), St Mary's Mt Abu, St Vincent's Asansol, St Edmund's Shillong, then onto St Mary's Orphanage, Dum Dum Calcutta.

Stories abound in the Indian Province about "the old rogue" (as the brothers would fondly call him). One characteristic of Br Dineen was his hospitality. He maintained excellent relations with lay people, the religious and clergy. His wit and charm and impish humour delighted any visitor.

Besides his brilliant story telling prowess, some other fascinating stories about him needs mention:

* At St Michael's Kurji, there was a shortage of beer at one stage. Br Dineen and a few Jesuits got together and started a home brew!!

* On being questioned why he had not been progressing in his spiritual reading, he retorted that such activities had driven certain Brothers insane!

* He had optical problems and had lost his sight in one eye. However, he loved to drive. In Kurseong there were a few narrow escapes, but this did not prevail upon him to relinquish the wheel until he had a close encounter with the rear of a truck! One of his passengers alleged that, at the time, he had the bad eye in Nepal and the good one in Bhutan!

His last regular posting was at St Edmunds School Shillong, where rheumatoid arthritis took its toll, and caused him much suffering. After St Edmund's School, he spent some time in St Mary's Orphanage School Dumdum Calcutta, when the Christian Brotherhood decided to send him back to Baldoyle, Ireland in 1988, to spend the rest of his retirement. Here too he was the life and soul of the community. He passed away at 11.00 am on 11 August 1993. He was simple, frank and forthright, uncluttered and devoid of any inner complications. His departure from India deprived the province not so much of a 'character' as a monument. Good times left with him, as did his infectious laughter, his message of hope, and his belief in the innate goodness of human beings.

Rest in Peace Beloved Sir!

Without such background information, our eulogy would lack its full measure. Bro Dineen was his Class Teacher in 1969-70. Shannonisms

By Mrinmoy Kanti Dasgupta & Roon Dowerah

Brother M.G. Shannon (1928–2012)

Brother M.G. Shannon (1928–2012) was one of the larger-than-life Irish Christian Brothers we had the good fortune of knowing during our school years. It was a common sight to see him on the campus, cassock sleeves rolled up, working in the gardens, trimming hedges. When he wasn't there with Basu—the famous Pagla Māli—or mowing the lawn, he was leaving unforgettable impressions on generations of Edmundians.

Boarders, especially, remember him vividly. At 6:00 a.m. sharp, he would stride down the dormitory aisle to wake us. And woe betide the boy who didn't get up—Brother Shannon would yank away the blankets and have the hapless fellow on the floor before he knew what happened.

Academically, he taught us Physics alongside Prof. N.N Ganguly (Jango). Renowned for his sharp wit, he transformed Physics lessons with his trademark rejoinders, lightning-fast repartee, and perfectly timed one-liners delivered with absolute deadpan seriousness. He would peer over the rim of his spectacles and deliver a line that left you standing there, wondering whether you had just been insulted or educated. His humour became so well known that, over the years, Edmundians formally recorded his sayings under the now-cherished title: "Shannonisms".

Here are a few favourites:

* During Physics class, when the room fell silent and someone—usually a back-bencher—shattered the calm with a loud yawn, he would slap his forehead, stand on his toes and peer dramatically into the offender's mouth:
"Aw, c'mon fella, you don't have to show me what you've had for breakfast!"
or
"Hey man, I can see right up to your toes!"

* "Aw c'mon, put your hooves in!" when he spotted someone stretching out his legs.

* "Keep those hooves quiet, will ya?" when restless feet shuffled under the desks.

* "Mind your paws!" for wandering hands.

* "Hey, remove that paw from your skull, will ya?!" when someone stood scratching his head, unable to answer.

* "Hey you fellas, on the expensive seats out there!" referring to the back-benchers.

* "Wipe that smile off your dial!"

* When a young boy once asked him why hair didn't grow on his head, Brother Shannon knelt to eye level and replied with mock seriousness: "Son, hair doesn't grow on brains."

* "Names of fools are like their faces, always seen in public places."

* "Hey you there, fella with the face, come here." (usually before a reprimand)

* "Don't laugh too loud, I can see the holes in your socks."

* He was also a skilled chalk-shooter. A yawning mouth in Physics class was a target—and he rarely missed. On one occasion, lacking chalk, he simply looked at the offender and said: "Hey you Gomm, I can see the holes in your socks through your mouth!"

And when someone laughed loudly at this, he added: "Why are you laughing like the backside of a bus?"

* "What's that head of yours meant for, to separate your two ears, eh?"

* "Like poles repel," when two boys quarrelled.

* "Yes, that's right, (as electrical current), you following the 'line of least resistance', eh?" when we skipped working steps in numerical answers.

* "Stand up on your hind legs!" when asking a student to rise.

* "Close your beak!" when requiring silence.

* "Lightning and thunder happen at the same time. If you see lightning but don't hear the thunder, it means you've been struck!"

* To a boy late on a rainy day without umbrella or raincoat: "You could have walked between the raindrops!"

* "If you are waiting for an auspicious day to start studying, well, today is a very auspicious day. And for Heaven's sake, keep your grandparents alive for the next two years!"

* "Get that paw away from your puss!" to someone listening too intently with a hand over his mouth.

* "Shut your gob, don't carry-on, you," his trademark command whenever things got noisy or argumentative.

* "Aw, away with ye," his favourite dismissal.

Even today, decades later, his words echo in reunions and conversations that begin with, "Do you remember when Brother Shannon said…?" What began as classroom humour has become shared nostalgia — a reminder of a time when learning was not only serious business, but also filled with laughter, surprise, and an occasional flying piece of chalk.

For many of us, these Shannonisms represent more than clever lines. They remind us of mornings in the dormitory, the smell of chalk dust, the creak of wooden desks, and the camaraderie of growing up together. They remind us of a teacher who could command silence with a look, spark laughter with a sentence, and challenge us with a question — all in the same breath.

In the end, what we remember is not only what he taught, but also how he made us feel: alert, amused, slightly terrified, but always deeply connected to a school and a man we still speak of with affection and respect.

His classes have long ended, but his words remain part of Edmundian memory and lore.

Edmundian Slang: A Glossary of Our Times

By Roon Dowerah

The language of schoolboy life at St. Edmund's

St. Edmund's School owes much of its distinctive slang to the Irish Christian Brothers who shaped its academic and cultural life. Their English was clear and readily understood, rarely revealing the idiomatic Irish English of their homeland; yet, Irish expressions occasionally surfaced. These expressions found their way into everyday school usage and, over the years, blended with local borrowings and Edmundian coinages. The result was a shared language of schoolboy life, familiar across generations.

Many of these expressions were first documented in the Edmundian Lexicon of the 1960s, compiled by Manju Barua (SEC 1962–1967) and published in 2005.

This glossary is a Reunion Edition, expanded to include additional slang phrases not recorded in the original work, while remaining faithful to the original work that sought to preserve Edmundian speech of the 1950s, 1960s, and 1970s.

Acting funny: Interrogative phrase meaning looking for a fight. "Acting funny, huh?"

After you: (request) to borrow an item once the current user is done with it (e.g., someone reading a comic). Often led to disputes over who said it first. Also used as a table request: "Dol, after you."

A you:
1. Used by a senior to address a junior whose name he doesn't know: "A you, come here.";
2. Used among peers when asserting authority: "A you, where's my geometry set?"

All balls: Exclamation meaning nonsense or lies. Also used as a verb phrase: "What he said is all balls."

As if: With a special intonation by juniors meaning:
1. "As if I don't know you're wrong.";
2. Don't expect me to believe that.

And a half: Strengthening suffix: "He's a fool and a half" = even more of a fool.

Away with ye: Irish dismissal meaning "I don't believe you." Used by Brothers, especially Bro. Shannon: "Aw, away with ye."

Aw, g'wan: Irish dismissive "Oh go on with you," meaning disbelief. Occasionally mimicked by students.

Backchat: Impertinent talk to seniors or prefects.

Backside: Posterior; buttocks.

Bad mood: Warning about a teacher's temper: "Bro Cooney's in a bad mood." Functioned as an alert for juniors to be cautious.

Balls
1. Nonsense or bragging.
2. Testicles (mildly objectionable).
3. Courage: "Does he have the balls?"
4. As verbal attack: "Balls to you."

Bashed up: Beaten up. Earlier term: lammed up.

Baths: Hot-water showers for boarders, taken twice weekly. A communal affair.

Benders: Punishment: bending forward so the teacher can cane the buttocks ("six of the best").

Belt: Irish slang for hit: "Come here, young fella, I'll belt you!"

Big Dad: Derogatory: someone acting important. "Getting to be a big dad, huh?"

Big Dorm: Dormitory for seniors (Class VI onwards).

Blackguard: Irish for "no-good fellow." Used only by the Brothers.

Bloody: Strengthening adjective. Considered impolite in Shillong society but heavily used in school. "You bloody" could be offensive or friendly. "Bloody bugga" usually affectionate; "bloody swine" more hostile.

Bluff: To deceive or lie. "Don't bluff me." Pre-dated the later "bullshit."

Boarders: Resident students.

Bogs: Irish term for toilets. School usage: long bogs (defecate), short bogs (urinate).

Bold: Irish English for "naughty." Used by Brothers before punishment: "You bold boy."

Box Room: Indoor area where boarders kept their clothes and toiletries in wooden lockers.

Bucks: Money; used like "one buck" instead of "one rupee." "Dough" used for money in general.

Buck up
1. Put in more effort.
2. Cheer up.

Bugga: Corruption of bugger:
1. Friendly term for "fellow."
2. An objectionable person: "lousy bugga."
3. Pity: "poor bugga."

Bum
1. Despicable person.
2. Lazy person ("lazy bum").
3. Buttocks.

Bumfat: In football, a hard hit from a close-range kick. Often intentional.

Bumps: Birthday "bumps": lifting and dropping a boy so his backside hits the ground once per year of age. Could be painful.

Bunk: To run away or be absent without permission. "He bunked off from Study Hall."

Buzz off: Go away.

Carry-on: Irish for commotion or argument. Used by Brothers: "Don't carry on now."

Chasing dames: Showing interest in a girl. "You're chasing that dame or what?"

Cheater cock: Games insult for foul play.

Chimti: Hindi for "pinch." In school: small or tiny: "chimti cutlet," "chimti faat."

Chimti faat: Non-entity; insignificant person. Related terms: fartlet, punk.

Chuck: To throw. Overused; replaced "throw."

Chucked out: Expelled—ultimate punishment. "If you fail again, you'll get chucked out."

Cockeyed: Crooked or not straight. "That line is cockeyed."

Cock-house: House with maximum games points; winning House party.

Cog: Copy schoolwork or cheat in exams.

Come on, men: Opening plea or coaxing phrase. Also used to calm tempers: "Come on, men, don't get angry, men."

Conked out: Break down, fail, become unconscious, or die. Used for all these.

Cop tate: Euphemism of copped it: to get into trouble. Also warning: "You cop tate, huh?" / "Cop tate—don't go out of bounds."

Copycat: One who copies behaviour or work, often taunted: "Copycat! Copycat!"

Corridors: Indoor area used for studies during Annual Concert when desks were removed from the Study Hall.

Cracked: Mad, eccentric, or having absurd ideas. "Are you getting cracked or what?"

Crackpot: Foolish or reckless person; used to warn against stupid risks: "Don't be a crackpot."

Dame: Girl. "Is she an LC or PM dame?"

Dayskie: A day scholar, as referred to by boarders.

Dhapped up: Slapped around; replaced the older lammed up. Entered usage in the late '60s.

Digging for gold: Picking one's nose.

Dirty: Obscene. "Don't say dirty words."

Dirty Goofy: PT Teacher Mr. Lama's trademark rebuke, cane in hand: "Dirty goofy! I'll break your hath!" Used by students only in mimicry.

Ditch
1. Leave.
2. Fail to show up or keep one's word: "That bugga always ditches men!" Also used for a girlfriend "giving the ditch."

Dodge: To evade or pretend. Common for skipping class, studies, or games: "Dodging games, huh?"

Dol please: Dining-table request: pass the dal jug. Became "dal please" by the late '70s.

Dope: Idiot.

Dough: Money.

Dumbkopf: Fool; idiot. Introduced by Mr. Ludwig (teacher 1965–66); first German addition to Edmundian slang

Exchange: Dining-table barter: "Anyone exchange one chop for four chips?"

Fags: Cigarettes.

Fagged out: Exhausted; worn out.

Fagging: Smoking cigarettes.

Fart around: Fool around.

Fat lump: Derogatory term for an overweight person.

Fella: Irish for "boy" or "male." Common with teachers: "Come here, young fella."

Fiddle: To touch or mess with something delicate: "Don't fiddle with the records." Also used for radio tuning.

Flicks: Movies; the cinema.

Flicked: Stole; pilfered.

Flog
1. Beat with a cane.
2. Defeat soundly in a game: "We got flogged."

Funk goose: Someone easily frightened; gutless.

Funnings: Marbles played for fun, not for stakes (reals).

GF: Girlfriend.

Gawk: Idiot (local use, not the Irish "stare").

Give us an intro: Introduce me to that girl.

Gorilla: Nickname for Discipline Teacher Mr Harry D'Santos (appointed 1969). Also code named "No. 73" from an animal-card lucky draw.

Gob / Gap: Irish for mouth. "Keep your gob shut." Used by Brothers and prefects.

Grub: Food; snacks; meals. "Got some grub?" / "Let's go for grub."

Have it out: Fight. "Let's have it out after 3 o'clock."

Heck: Mild exclamation for annoyance or oversight. "What the heck?" Used instead of the stronger word almost never spoken then.

High hopes: Taunt or dismissive reply: "High hopes you('ve) got."

Hog: Eat. "Come, let's hog."

Hogging: Eating secretly or on the sly.

Hooked out: Went out of bounds; sneaked off (often to Downshop for a fag).

Hospi: School hospital, with separate quarantine ward for chickenpox/measles/mumps.

Hot: Sexy; attractive: "That hot dame from LC."

I'll see you: Warning of future retaliation: "I'll see you at the khud side."

Iondom: "Iron marble"—old ball bearings used in marbles for superior striking power.

J: Jealous: "Don't get J."

Jazzy: Stylish attire—drainpipes, pointed shoes, Beatle boots.

Juicy ball: Cricket slang for a slow, hittable full toss or short ball.

Keds: Canvas shoes.

Khud Side: Slope at the edge of First Field; usual spot for settling scores after 3 p.m.

LC: Loreto Convent.

Lammed up: Beaten up; older form of bashed up or dhapped up. From Irish "lamped him."

Lazy bum: Lazy fellow.

Like nobody's business: To an extreme degree: "Cogging like nobody's business."

Long sleep: Morning wake-up call for St. Edmund's School boarders was at 5.45 am, followed by a forty-five-minute study period before breakfast. On holidays (not Sundays) wake-up call was given at 6:30 am with study time dropped — an unannounced late start called a "long sleep."

Lousy: Bad; e.g., lousy flick, lousy grub. Lousy bugga = worthless fellow.

Ma Fandi: Nickname for Miss G Fernandes, Commissariat (food service manager). She was also our Class III (Sec A) Teacher.

Malacca: Mythical cane (from Malacca) — the ever-feared school cane.

Meangie: Stingy. Meangie bagga = stingy fellow.

Men: Casual suffix in speech: "No, men" / "Ya, men."

Messed (in) his pants: Euphemism for having defecated in one's pants (used by junior dorm ayah).

Mugging: Studying hard.

My (left) foot!: Dismissive exclamation of denial.

Night bogs: Toilets beside dorms for use during sleep hours (junior/senior separate).

No men: Anglo-Indian "no." (Opposite: ya men.)

No Use: Means "no good." "No use fighting, men."

Oiler: Obsequious student who curries favour with teachers or seniors.

Out of bounds: Outside the SEC campus limits.

Own: Expression of superiority: "I own you at carroms."

Pocker: Pocket Money,

PM: Pine Mount School.

Piddle: Urinate; short bogs; also urine.

Pictures: Movies — school films shown on Wednesday nights.

Playing the fool: Acting foolishly; admonishment: "Don't play the fool." Can mean not behaving or trying to bluff.

Plugged: Failed an exam. "I plugged, men." Old-school British and Irish school slang. In late 19th –early 20th century British military slang, a soldier who performed badly or was punished, could be described as "plugged"—as in hit, rejected, or marked down.

Poaching: (Hockey/football) Opportunist forward hanging around near the goal.

Pocket billiards: Auto-eroticism via trouser pocket (adolescent slang).

Punk
1. Nonentity.
2. Cheeky junior ("You chimti punk").

Ragged: Angry; to lash out.

Reals: Marbles played for stakes (opposite funnings). If you lose, shug up your marbles.

Saw stars: Hit so hard you momentarily black out or see stars; also used metaphorically: "The paper was so tough I saw stars, men!"

Scram: Dismissive "get out of sight" command.

Shagged out: Worn out, broken; shag also used for masturbate.

Shug up: Pay up.

Shucks: Mild exclamation for disappointment or embarrassment: "Oh shucks", "shucks, men"

Side-dish: Saturday dinner item (mutton with veg/soy sauce — "soya dish").

Slick: Good quality; antonym of lousy. Predecessor to jazzy/groovy.

Smarty pants: Irritatingly clever person; often taunted by lady teachers: "Come here, you smarty pants!"

Sneak: Snitch; informer. "He's a bloody sneaker."

Snot: Nasal mucus.

Socials: Official co-ed parties for seniors; school-hosted socials (with LC and PM). Discontinued in 1974.

Soiree: Evening cultural event held in the Study Hall, term popularised by Rev. Bro. Brendan MacCarthaigh, who composed the St. Edmund's School Anthem for the Golden Jubilee celebrations in 1965.

Stamps: Game term: a call that prevents an opponent from changing a move (e.g., chess) or has marbles implications.

Stand: Treat; pay for others. "Who's standing?" (Opposite of going Dutch.)

Stone Building: The stone-masonry structure east of the old swimming pool. Constructed in 1942 by the U.S. Army, it originally served as an operation theatre when the school was taken over during World War II and converted into a military hospital.

Studies: Time to be in the Study Hall.

Summer House: Gazebo south of the school building.

Swear: Used in exclamation "I swear!" meaning, I fully agree with you.

Takes a grudge: To hold a lasting grievance against a student: "He takes a grudge on me." (Related: take spite.)

Taking rounds: Brothers moving round the buildings or senior students taking long walks around campus (senior field → gate → college → scholasticate → swimming pool → tennikoit court).

Taw: Large marble used to strike smaller marbles;

Thinkso: Variant of "Think so"; used mid-sentence as reflective "I thinkso I better study or as a challenge: "Thinkso I can't bash you?"

Tickle tickle: Dismissive response to a weak joke, accompanied by a mock armpit-tickle gesture.

Thugging: Robbing or raiding — e.g., stealing day-scholars' tiffin boxes.

Togs: Swimming trunks; also trousers. "Jazzy togs, men."

Town: Police Bazar — cinemas, restaurants; Saturday walks via Jacob's Ladder.

Tucks: Food brought from home.

Tuck shop: School canteen (southwest corner of the Stone Building until early '80s).

Under foundry: Space under wooden floors supported by brick columns.

Vamoose: Get lost quickly — from spaghetti-western cowboy films.

Want it?: Intimidating "do you want a punch?" gesture, often shown with a clenched fist. (Local: mungta lia.)

What the hell?: Exclamation of disbelief or irritation: "What the hell, men — no flicks today." A key Edmundian catchphrase.

Walks: Saturday afternoon outing for boarders (Class VI+), to Police Bazar.

Wallop
1. Hit hard.
2. Defeat badly.

Ya men: Anglo-Indian "yes" (counterpart: no men).

Yahoo: Exclamation of joy (from the 1960s film Junglee). "No school tomorrow, yahoo men!"

Yap: Incessant chatter. "Shut your yap or I'll belt ya!"

Zuck: Nothing; zero (orig. U.S.).

Echoes from the Hills: My Years at St. Edmund's School Shillong

By Pius Alphonso Sir

Walking through the corridors of a school for the first time is always a mixture of excitement and nervousness. Entering St. Edmund's School, Shillong, in 1969 as a young teacher marked the beginning of an unforgettable chapter—one that would shape both my professional life and leave memories I cherish to this day.

During my years at St. Edmund's, I had the joy and responsibility of guiding Classes VII and VIII, teaching boys from all over North East India. Each brought his own energy, culture, and dreams into the classroom, and managing this diversity was both a challenge and a delight.

It was during my tenure at St. Edmund's that I met my present wife, June Lawrence, a colleague, and finally we had the joy of getting married in the little school chapel—a fact that binds my life forever to that stellar institution.

The bonds I formed with colleagues and mentors remain as vivid as ever. My fellow teachers and the school leadership provided guidance, support, and camaraderie that shaped my growth as an educator. St. Edmund's was far more than a workplace; it was a close-knit community bound by shared values, faith, and purpose. The spirit of the school—instilling integrity, responsibility, and compassion—left a lasting impression on all who passed through its gates.

Though I will not be part of this reunion, my thoughts and warm good wishes are very much with all who gather to relive those cherished years. Time may have carried us along different paths, but the lessons learned and friendships formed at St. Edmund's remain timeless.

May this reunion rekindle old friendships, revive treasured memories, and strengthen the enduring Edmundian spirit. I wish the school continued growth and grace, and all its alumni lives enriched by the values first nurtured on that blessed hill in Shillong.

— Pius Alphonso

(Class VII B Teacher 1972 and Class VIII A Teacher 1973)

Memories of St. Edmunds School Shillong

By Sanjoy Sarma

Going down memory lane after 50 years is challenging especially since one tends to either gloss over the negatives or create a reality which is far from the truth. I hope I have managed to avoid both these pit falls while penning down my most vivid memories of school.

The first clear memory of the school was the walk to school from Risa Colony. Climbing the stairs to come to see, in awe, what would be my second home for the next 6 years. The gazebo on the right as one walked up the stairs seemed to be a very imposing structure.

One of the greatest things that happened to one at school was meeting new friends who remained friends throughout a lifetime. It was a little awkward for me as I was a Hindi speaking kid (coming from Delhi) surrounded by English speaking children with albeit multiple accents reflecting the true cosmopolitan nature of our school.

Now for the juicier titbits. Smoking started very early in Shillong. I still remember the first puff of a cigarette at "Down Shop", a haunt for most students. What my friend did not tell me was that one was not to inhale deeply on the first puff. The Charminar cigarette and my constitution were at odds and I just passed out. only to be woken up some time later, and I saw my more veteran friends hiding their giggles. However, now I had graduated and had become one of them.

There was however, the nagging fear of being caught out by my family so one spent a lot of time to get the ideal combination of leaves and kwai that worked as an effective mask.

As one climbed the steps of progress and reached Class 6, my next experiment was to try "sadha". My friend gave me a demo but forgot to mention that it should not touch the intersection of the teeth and gum. I got knocked out again and thankfully recovered before being noticed.

The hikes that we did with our class teacher were not only excellent experiences in themselves but the fact that we learnt to admire the flora and fauna, especially butterflies, was a fantastic memory.

The Walkathon to Barapani (or Umiam as it's called now) as part of a charity event that had Mother Teressa made a lasting impression. Of course, the presence of the girls made the walkathon go like a breeze.

As the capital shifted to Dispur my parents decided that I should stay back in boarding school and so Class 9 & 10 were spent as a Boarder.

In Class 8, my parents decided that I should be their child who should have Assamese as a second language instead of Hindi. This was a tectonic shift. However, with friends like Roon and Amiya, one crossed that barrier too.

Class 9 and 10 were made especially interesting, thanks to the school authorities deciding that we would be the first ICSE batch. Back in those days "stress and anxiety" were unheard of so we all took it in our strides without even a whimper of protest.

Hostel life was interesting. One of the quirks in school was that hot water was made available only 2 days in a week and that meant that after the evening of foot ball or hockey one would head to the showers start singing and jump into the cold water. After a few numbing seconds the water became bearable.

The sessions of hand ball were made interesting by a few girls passing the court and at that time whoever was on court became a superman, I am sure none of the fair maidens even noticed. However, surprisingly we received some not so gentle reminders to keep far away from the girls, but I guess we really couldn't care less.

The exams came and went and then it was time to pack our bags and head home. It did pull at the heartstrings as some of us knew that we wouldn't be back in Shillong but for the many who came back to St. Edmunds college for their Pre University, it was Deja vu.

Down Memory Lane

By Pranab Misra

Fifty years. Fifty years is a long time, when a youth becomes a senior citizen, a student becomes a professor, a young man from listening to sermons gives sermons to younger generations, when student leaders become leaders of mass movements. Yes, fifty years is a long time.

And fifty years have gone by since my batch passed out of school.

A few days back, Kunal asked me whether I could contribute an article for the magazine to be released on the occasion of the 50th anniversary of our passing ICSE.

I was unsure. I had not been in touch with any of my classmates since leaving school. Kunal was the only friend whom I had met during his visit to Bhubaneswar. And there were rare conversations over phone with a few. Shillong had been a forgotten place for me with no connection other than a few photos.

As I sat alone and started thinking, memories of nearly five decades back, slowly and gradually, came flooding by brighter with each passing day. My earliest memories of Shillong were of the mid-sixties, 1965 to be precise, when my father was first posted there. Shillong of the mid-sixties was a thinly populated place with not many private vehicles plying apart from town buses. While driving up to Shillong, there was someplace called Tokola Pahar, which I cannot recollect properly. The surroundings were lush green, with Assam-type houses all around. There were hills and pine trees and streams flowing. A far cry from the Shillong of present day!

We stayed in Laban and I studied in Shishu Mandir school at Bishnupur for a year, before entering the hallowed portals of St-Edmunds in 1966, where Mrs Wallang was my first teacher in class 1. By then, we had shifted residence to Arbuthnot Road in Laitumkhrah, where we stayed in the house of one Mr J P Duara in 1966.

Apart from the morning assembly addressed by Brother Hayes, the Principal, both in 1966 and the following year, no special memories of school exist, other than catching tadpoles, playing marbles at school and collecting cones from pine trees.

Outside school, life was fun. Our landlord's grandson, Santanu was three years my senior and a good friend. As were the siblings of U T Bhuyan, who belonged to the 1970 batch ISC. His younger brother, Nilim, was a year junior and sadly is no more. Two other Edmundians, Sushil and Sudhir Trivedi stayed nearby. Late afternoons were fun-filled occasions spent playing and reading comics. Our house was a treasure trove of the comics of the sixties, Phantom, Tarzan, Walt Disney, Turok, Bugs Bunny, Bonanza, Black Hood...whew, the list is endless. I have retained most of my comics, purchased from Modern Book Depot, to this day.

Trips to Police Bazaar during weekends with Dad was a treat, which began with Modern Book Depot for purchase of comics and ended with Regal restaurant to relish it's famous dosas. And, of course, the jalebis of Delhi Mistan Bhandar. Laban Diary was another place from which dad brought edibles. Visits to Ward's Lake, Shillong Peak, Elephant Falls and watching movies at Anjalee, Bijou, Kelvin and Singhania theatres was a favourite pastime. The Sound of Music and Milan were both enjoyed at Anjalee. Dreamland came up later.

Winters were never spent in Shillong. I looked forward to the holidays from December to March, where we visited our native place in Odisha.

Come 1968 and I became a boarder in class three under the watchful eyes of Miss Lipika Dutta, with whom I am in touch to this day. Unlike life as a day scholar, life as a boarder was more chequered with a variety of activities. Meal timings, studies in the mornings and evenings were regulated and of course, the games and sports. I was in O'Brien house in 1968. Hosteliering introduced me to pocket money, importance of games and sports, camaraderie and prayers during meals and at bedtime, both of which I have practiced to this day.

Our generation literally hero-worshipped seniors like Anthony Duncan, Athikho Kathipri, Basant Bordoloi, Surinder Anand, Siddhartha Deb, Rakesh Gupta, to name a few, and cheered their sporting prowess.

We enjoyed watching football matches played by seniors and the Brothers. In one particular match between the Brothers and a team from Air Force played in the main ground with several bystanders, the Air Force team literally toyed with the Brothers, already up 4-0, before the spectators asked for the Brothers' goalkeeper, perhaps Brother MacCarthaigh, to be replaced by a student, Rakesh Gupta. Final result in favour of Air Force 6-2.

Swimming sessions, hiking and weekly walks were part of our activities. I must say the best way to move around Shillong is by walking.

During our evening studies, once I was clandestinely reading comics and chided by the monitoring teacher, Miss Remedius, who took away the comics. I was crestfallen, comics being among my prized possessions like any youngster. My joy knew no bounds as all comics taken by Miss Remedius from several students were returned just before final exams.

It was in 1968 when Robert Kennedy was assassinated. There were placards at the school main entrance mentioning the unfortunate event. Another event took place during the same time. Some students attempted to set some parts of the school on fire, were apprehended and expelled. A case of misguided youths!

It is with amusement that I reflect upon as to how my laundry and towels with my name stitched on them, always vanished from the boxes allotted to boarders for keeping clothes, and returned after a few days.

Nine months passed and it was goodbye to Shillong as father was transferred to Delhi in early 1969. New environment, new school, new teachers and new friends.

St-Columba's in Delhi, another institution run by the Irish Christian Brothers, where I joined in class 4, was a different set-up. The "Janta-nahin-mera-baap-kaun-hai" attitude, a mixture of arrogance and snobbery, prevailed among some students. Years later, Brother Oman, Brother Pinto and Brother Eric D'Souza and Brother Gaffney would head this institution.

Mike Nampui and Anjan Barkataki from Edmund's were my classmates at Columba's. Ninong Ering was there too, but senior. Life in Delhi was completely different from the one at Shillong.

Five years later, in March 1974, I re-entered Edmunds in class nine. My family was based first at Kench's Trace, where Jayant Rajan also stayed, and later we moved over to Assam Rifles.

We were seniors now with several youngsters looking up to us.

The classes of Brother D'Souza (Dasu) who was our class teacher, Jango (Physics), Menezes (Chemistry), Sen (Biology), Jain (Hindi), Brother Cooney (Maths) and Brother Dineen (Moral Science) were interesting. Dasu's humour, wit and academic delivery was matchless, sometimes even singing and playing the guitar in class! He taught us "The Merchant of Venice," and of course, History and Geography. He left at the end of the year. Mr Jain's classes inevitably had us gazing at his hands inside his trouser pockets!

It was a poignant feeling, hearing of Dasu's suffering during the final years of his earthly existence and his departure in 2024. I had met his dad, Major-General Eustace D'Souza, who had visited St-Columba's in 1973.

All teachers were highly supportive and helpful. Teaching for them was never a mere duty, rather an act of love and commitment. Unfortunately, as in life, several of our teachers are no longer around. It would be wonderful if the few who are still there could be with our batch during the celebrations.

Apart from Brother Oman, who taught us "The Rhyme of the Ancient Mariner," and who was our class teacher from March to June 1975, Brother Gaffney took charge thereafter till our finals in December 1975.

The games on the handball court during lunchtime, with Shaibal, Soumitra and Bardhan were enjoyable, with Mr Roca joining us sometimes.

The annual sports were a gala affair with seniors like Wanbok Star and Ronald Russell giving sterling displays in most events.

One (un)forgettable event occurred a few weeks before our finals, perhaps in September 1975. A friend of mine, not an Edmundian, and I decided to go to Barapani one morning, by my Atlas cycle, which I had used extensively in Delhi. It was enjoyable, but risky while driving downhill, almost colliding with a dhobi with clothes piled on his head. We reached Barapani by around 9 am and planned to start the return journey by 1pm. A few minutes into our return journey, double disaster struck. There was a puncture. And then the rains came. With no mobiles, no phone booths and no cycle repair shops during those days, we walked the entire journey taking turns to push the cycle up right from Barapani to Laitumkhrah.

My last meeting with my batchmates was after the final exams when we had gathered at Anjalee cinema to watch Lawrence of Arabia.

I shall always remember our teachers with gratitude and friends with nostalgia.

I shall cherish eating plums, nashpati and squash, which were unique. Travelling in the small town buses, enjoying the sound of droplets of rains on our roofs, going to school in gumboots, watching mother buy vegetables from Khasi women, called kongs, who had huge conical baskets slung from their foreheads and uttered something like Thek-Quai, visiting Cherrapunji (Sohra)...... priceless memories of childhood!

Two doctors whom I fondly remember are Dr Sehgal and Dr Borgohain, who was an eye specialist. His son Roktim was a friend of mine.

The school had provided us a springboard for greater laurels in life. Great institution, excellent teachers, lifelong camaraderie and the scenic beauty of Shillong...this concoction was heady enough to intoxicate us. What more can one desire during schooldays?

The winds of change have not spared Shillong, as I gather from the media. Shillong was a fairytale hill station in the sixties. I shall forever treasure the memories of the Shillong of the sixties and seventies in my heart and memories. If I were to stay in Shillong today, I would be another stranger.

You can take a person out of Shillong, but you cannot take Shillong out of the person. Some day, hopefully in the near future, I shall return to Shillong.

These past few days have virtually made me relive the days of my youth in Shillong. My heart and prayers go out to those classmates who are no longer in this world. With the era of reunions and alumni meets happening all across the country and overseas, it is a poignant and emotional moment for all participants to come together for a brief shining moment after decades. What more can I tell my batchmates other than it's YESTERDAY once more!

I wish our batch's golden jubilee celebrations good luck.

Fifty Years On: Memories of St. Edmund's (1966–1976)

By Bodhibrata Nag

It is hard to believe that fifty years have passed since we walked out of the gates of St. Edmund's as students for the last time. Yet the moment I sit down to write, the years fold back effortlessly, and the familiar smells, sounds, and sights of our school rise before me with a vividness that surprises me even today. Those ten years—from Class 1 to Class 10—were not just a phase of life; they were the mould that shaped us, the backdrop against which we discovered the world and ourselves.

My earliest memory of school is a slightly comical one. In Class 1, we had what was called "sleeping period"—a quiet hour in which we were supposed to rest on tiny mattresses. I remember trying very hard to fall asleep but failing miserably. Feeling deeply concerned, I reported to my teacher that I thought something was wrong with my heart because I simply could not sleep on command. The teacher's gentle, amused reassurance is still fresh in my mind. It was my first lesson in the fact that life cannot always be timed or ordered—not even by school timetable.

By Class 4, the school began to feel like a world I belonged to. I still remember the immense pride I felt when I was selected to present an elocution piece during the Gandhi Centenary Concert. For days, I practiced my lines with great seriousness, imagining myself on stage under the bright lights. That year I discovered something magical: the "light and shadow" method of storytelling. At home, with the help of a table lamp and some cut-outs, I staged my own performances for my parents, feeling like a great dramatist in the making.

The weather in Shillong was a character in our school lives. The rain—relentless, playful, unpredictable—was both friend and foe. I distinctly recall plodding through sheets of rain, soaked to the bone, only to reach school and discover that a holiday had been declared. The absurdity of trudging through a storm only to return home was somehow delicious at that age. We took pride in our endurance, even if it was misdirected.

Nature, in fact, was our constant companion. During lunch breaks, many of us became amateur naturalists. I remember fishing for tadpoles in the small pools around campus, storing them in jam bottles, and waiting with bated breath for them to turn into frogs. The metamorphosis fascinated us more than any biology textbook. Little did we know that we were unknowingly learning the principles of life cycles and ecosystems long before they appeared in our syllabus.

The long winter holidays were another world altogether. That was when I discovered the intoxicating charm of books. Enid Blyton and Biggles became my faithful companions, transporting me into worlds full of mystery, adventure, and heroism. Those stories shaped my imagination and taught me that the world was larger, stranger, and more wondrous than our classroom windows could show.

Every December, the final exams would come and go, and with them came a peculiar ritual. I would sit with the question papers and calculate how many more years I had before I could finally go to college—an oddly futuristic exercise for a schoolboy. Perhaps it was the natural impatience of youth or the thrill of imagining a life beyond familiar walls.

But the school gave us more than academics. It gave us beauty. I will never forget the sight of the Himalayas shimmering under the summer sun, visible on rare, perfectly clear mornings. The distant peaks, bathed in gold, made even the most restless of us pause. Many of us might not have understood it then, but those moments planted a quiet reverence for nature that would remain with us for life.

Around Class 7 or 8, our curiosity pushed us into the college biology lab during lunch hours. We would tiptoe in to examine the specimens suspended in formaldehyde, part horrified and part fascinated. It was a forbidden thrill—the kind that only schoolchildren understand.

Of course, not all our experiments were cerebral. My attempts at papier-mâché and drawing in Classes 5 and 6 were nothing short of disastrous. No amount of effort could coax beauty out of those misshapen figures and lopsided sketches. But the teachers persisted in encouraging us, demonstrating a patience that I appreciate far more today than I did back then.

The annual concerts remain among my fondest memories. The thrill of costumes, rehearsals, backstage chaos, and the applause of the audience created a magic that continues to glow even now. For days after the concert, we would relive the excitement, discussing who forgot their lines, who performed brilliantly, and whose costume fell apart at the wrong moment.

Then came the formative years—the years that prepared us not just for exams, but for life. Prof. Nripendra Nath Ganguly, our physics teacher, introduced us to a world where logic and wonder coexisted. His classes were a performance in themselves—complete with dramatic demonstrations and, occasionally, flying dusters aimed at inattentive students. But behind the theatrics lay a deep love for physics that was contagious.

Prof. Francis Menezes, our chemistry teacher, was a man of quiet brilliance. He passed away just as we were entering college, leaving behind a legacy of rigorous teaching and gentle humour. His practical lessons, especially the mysterious world of chemical reactions, formed the backbone of our later scientific pursuits.

In history and geography, Brother Eric D'Souza was unmatched. He turned every lesson into a spellbinding narrative, weaving facts with imagination in ways that made the past come alive and the world seem achievable. It is no surprise that these subjects remain close to my heart even today.

And then there was Brother Pinto, whose renditions of Shakespeare and Wordsworth transformed poetry into something vivid, emotional, and unforgettable. He made us see that literature wasn't merely to be studied—it was to be felt. Even now, when I read those poets, I hear his voice delivering those timeless lines.

Finally came the defining milestone of our school life—the ICSE examinations, held at Loreto's. For us, Loreto was a territory of both awe and adolescent intrigue. Coupled with our anxiety at being the first ICSE batch, we felt uniquely unprepared, as if history had played a trick on us. But we survived, and, in the process, learned more about ourselves than any textbook could teach.

Fifty years later, what remains is not the anxiety of exams or the weight of homework, but the laughter, the friendships, the teachers, the rain-soaked mornings, the magic of concerts, the smell of books, the grandeur of the mountains, and the peculiar adventures that only a school like St. Edmund's could offer.

Time has carried us far, but St. Edmund's remains our shared beginning—our anchor, our refuge, and the place where we learned to dream. As we celebrate our Golden Jubilee, I feel grateful for every memory, every teacher, every friendship, and every moment that shaped us into who we are today.

Here's to the school that gave us roots—and the passage of fifty years that taught us how precious those roots truly are.

Accidental Tryst with a Wig and a Gown

By Vijay Konwar

From the 1974-75 Annual School Operettas

Among the many fun memories jostling for elbow room in my mind, two incidents regularly fight their way to the top— my accidental rise to stardom in St. Edmund's back in 1974 and '75.

They sparkle with such enduring brilliance that they still make me smile at random moments—while driving, while walking in the park, or occasionally during those rare, glorious moments of solitude when no one is asking me to do anything.

ACT I: From Thief to Wife (A Promotion Nobody Asked For)

It was September 1974—if my memory serves me right, which, at my age, is always a bold assumption. Rehearsals were in full frenzied swing for the school annual operetta: Ali Baba and the 40 Thieves. I had been selected as one of the 40 thieves, a role that demanded nothing more strenuous than standing around looking menacing.

One day, when the "extras" (a polite term for "useless") were not needed, a classmate and I snuck into the auditorium to spy on the main cast. One of my classmates was masquerading as Mrs. Cassim—the wife of Ali Baba's wealthy, greedy elder brother. Feeling slightly mischievous (and clearly unaware of the karmic consequences), I whispered to my friend that I could do a better job as Mrs. Cassim.

I should've kept my mouth shut. The universe loves such moments.

Twenty days before the play, the boy playing Mrs. Cassim went down with jaundice. The school went into panic mode. My friend, loyal as a traitor, tattled to Br Beddoe, the director, about my "I can do it better" comment.

Br Beddoe didn't waste a second. He grabbed me, hauled me to the stage, made me audition, and then announced, like an emperor passing judgment:

"YOU are Mrs. Cassim."

Just like that, my thieving career ended and I was promoted—promoted! —to becoming someone's wife.

My initial anxiety evaporated once I was swept up in a whirlwind of rehearsals. Suddenly, I was memorising dialogues, singing songs, and being trained by a very patient pianist who worked harder on tuning me than the piano. She kept saying, "Follow the note!" while I kept wondering where the note went.

ACT II: The Makeover That Defied Science

Since I wasn't exactly blessed in the looks department, I genuinely felt sorry for the brave souls in charge of my makeover. They had to work extra hard—

• procuring a wig that didn't slide off my head,
• designing a flowing gown that concealed more than it revealed,
• tailoring it to my unconventional shape,
• and spending hours on makeup, trying to convert "schoolboy" into "reasonably presentable woman."

Frankly, they deserved a medal. Their task was less makeover and more miracle.

ACT III: D-Day — The Rise of a Very Unexpected Star

All their hard work paid off on D-day. To my utter shock, every time I stepped onto the stage, the packed hall erupted in cheers and applause. During a particularly funny scene, I spotted my mother, my sisters, and my aunt completely in splits. Whether they were laughing at the joke or at the sight of me dressed like a wealthy lady—I still don't know, and nobody is telling me.

And thus, I experienced my first taste of the glorious but fleeting fame that came from standing under bright arc lights while dressed as someone's wife.

The funniest side-effect?

A few days later, I actually received a letter from a girl in a neighbouring school saying she wanted to be friends.

Imagine my confusion:

Of all the roles I could've played to impress girls, it had to be a woman?!

Not a prince, not a warrior…

No. A wife with a wig.

ACT IV: The Sequel That Sealed My Fate

The next year, the school staged Aladdin and Out, and by then my reputation as "Edmund's Most Reliable Stage Woman" was firmly established. I was the predictable choice to play Mrs. Mustafa—Aladdin's aunt. This time, the costume department gave me an upgrade. I was transformed into the elegant wife of a wealthy man. My previous year's wig, I was told, was consigned to obscurity.

ACT V: The Song That Followed Me Into Adulthood

But the best part—the part that has stayed with me forever—was a song I performed alongside a chorus of "girls," who were actually junior school boys forced into ribbons and frills:

"Take this advice, I have it from a friend!
Marry only gentlemen on whom you can depend!
The dearest may be cheapest in the end!"

Ironically, this is exactly the kind of advice I now give my daughter—proof that wisdom can sometimes arrive dressed in a long gown, wearing smudged lipstick, and belting out lines under a stage light.

Life comes full circle.

At least in the realm of memory.

Sometimes in a wig.

Sometimes in a gown.

But always with a good laugh!

My Roller Coaster ride from Class II to Class X in St Edmund's

By Mrinmoy Kanti Dasgupta

1. PROLOGUE

It was springtime of 1967, March to be precise. It was with mixed feelings that I had embarked on a Willys Jeep with my folks from the sleepy town of Goalpara (where my Dad got transferred from Shillong in December 1966) for an arduous eight hour journey, with a lunch stopover in Guwahati, and reached the hallowed portals of St Edmund's for the first time: such feelings emanated from the fact that (a) fundamentally I was a Shillongite in my pre-Edmund's years, having studied in St Peter's for three years, from KG to Class I, which, for me, was an easy commute from my ancestral house in Dhanketi, and I was happy returning to my beloved hometown after a period of just three months (b) anguish of being separated from my parents and younger brother for the next seven months (till Puja hols), and of course, (c) trepidation of being catapulted into the boarding school environment for the first time in my life.

2. Phase I

But surprisingly, I adapted expeditiously to the new environment. My first memory of the school was being escorted to the Junior Dorm in the evening of our first day, and given custody to the Dormitory Matrons (two sisters and three kongs). The dorm was teeming with boarders of the junior classes, the "freshers" like me apprehensive of what lay in store for us, and the "veterans" were in a boisterous mood, as there was so much to catch up with their school friends after the three months winter hols. Of course, often a "veteran" would tease another of his ilk, and say: "Look, he is homesick already!" On hearing this, I wondered what sort of disease was this! My parents handed over my tucks and trunk containing my attire, toiletries and other appurtenances to the Matrons, and I was set to go! Last minute hugs from my parents, with my promises of writing letters regularly, and then we were marched to the Dining Hall for dinner.

My Trunk, with the lettering painted painstakingly by my Dad, which was with me throughout my Boarding School Years. It remains in my attic to this day, a vestige of my past.

And thus was the beginning of my four years as a Boarder from Class II to Class V. Some remembrances of this period were:

• Football Discovery: Playing football for the first time, I realised that I could kick only with my left foot, while almost everyone else were right-footed; I thought I was abnormal, only to realise that this was my strength, as "lefties" were hard to come by, and I secured my position comfortably in either left back or left wing, and my left footers were well known (some of you may remember!)

• Public Letter Reading: The letters that we received from our folks were received by the Junior Dorm sisters, and were read out aloud in public to us before bed time! What an embarrassment!

• Pocket Money: Monthly pocket money was Rs 5/- in Classes II and III, and went up to Rs 10/- in Classes IV and V; we used to get a pro rata weekly dole out by Bro DM Cooney: there were three means of lightening your pocket – 1. The Tuck Shop which operated from 12 noon to 1 pm lunch time, and again in the evening between games and study times (there was a mind boggling variety of confectioneries and of course, the unique so called "lemonade": I could never figure out the formula of this carbonated beverage (the "lemonade" machine was operated by a Christian Brother) till the mid-nineties, when I chanced upon this green coloured syrupy drink without carbonation – Eureka!, the school days "lemonade" was actually Ralli's Green Mango syrup, diluted and carbonated!) 2. During Sundays and other holidays, some of us would go to the lower field boundary, and coax the chowkidar at the border to get us pork chow or big pork momos at 50p per plate – invariably such items came wrapped in a leaf or newspaper from nearby Hill Restaurant 3. The college canteen run by Jamuna, where mouth-watering mutton parathas at 50p and Alu chops at 25p were the rage.

• The School Hospital: We got our regular dose of cough syrups and other concoctions in the mornings after breakfast in the "OPD" section as well as the mandatory vaccinations. I was twice "incarcerated" in the "IPD" section, which had two rooms (one the general ward, and the other, the quarantine ward, which housed chicken pox, mumps, and measles patients: in 1969, I contracted chicken pox, and in 1970, measles: I suspect that the swimming pool had something to do with these infections. But it was fun in the quarantine ward – I used to get a good stock of tuck and loads of story books and comics, mostly Classics Illustrated and Classics Junior from my folks. It was fun in the ward, with pillow fights and all, after the Sister in Charge, withdrew at bed time! Some seniors in the General Ward used to "hook out" on Wednesday evenings to watch the movies!

• Leisurely Activities: Other than reading comics, playing marbles, hopscotch, "kings" (a ball game), "steps" (another ball game), we used to (a) play Cowboys and Indians on the slopes off First Field (bordering on Jowai Road): we collected pine needles for making our camps, and made "taws" from the clay there! (b) run our "Dinky" toy cars down the sloping drain next to the steps adjacent to the khud side between First Field and the adjacent lower field (c) embark on a "treasure hunt" in the Under Foundry, but I remember my findings to be damaged commodes and cisterns (Yech!) (c) go on hikes escorted by some Brother uphill via the Fruit Garden Road, catching butterflies, and collecting the red and purple wild berries in our hankies, and we squeezed out the juice during lunch time into our tumblers (d) catching tadpoles in pickle and jam bottles during the rainy season.

• Me as Arithmetic Paper Examiner! This was after the 2nd Term exams in 1968 (Class III). My Class Teacher, Miss Fernandez (popularly called Ma Fandi) was my Class Teacher in Sec A. She sent word to me on this half day / Sunday, that I was to meet her: and hence, with some trepidation, I proceeded to her room just behind the School Hospital, wondering if there was anything amiss. Having negotiated my way through Cindy, her dachshund, and Butch a terrier, I was welcomed by her in a very friendly manner: I must have done excellent in Arithmetic, as she asked me to evaluate the 2nd Term arithmetic papers of Sec B guys! While the general Boarding fraternity had their bland bread, jam and tea at 3pm and then moved off for Games, the Birthday party commenced thereafter on a single dining table and we were treated to a delectable assortment of goodies. It's not that every boarder could host such treats, as that depended on their "pocker" status.

• Deaths of two beloved Irish Brothers in 1970: Two of the most gentle Irish Brothers (Rev Bros O'Neill and JC Tuohy) left for their heavenly abode in quick succession in October and November. Doing Something Together as a Team

By Dr Amiya Kumar Sharma

We are all excited to be meeting as a group after fifty years or so. Such a meeting is bound to be special because although we are not young to be even in college, our mindset would almost be like that of school kids coming together again. Meeting a classmate whom we haven't met for fifty years evokes feelings that are completely different, even though we were together for less than ten years or so. Our ranks and achievements will not matter - at least let us hope that they won't – and we will meet like we were once: joyful, playful school kids where parental status and positions never mattered.

My thoughts here concern the period after our meeting ends. I am looking at creating a group for doing something for the underprivileged. Our beloved teacher Br Eric D'Souza had set an example for us to follow. I am sure some of us are already engaged in similar efforts. One doesn't have to contribute financially – simply being part of the group itself is important. I am sure that, together, we can generate innovative approaches to help the underprivileged sections of society.

I am part of the RGVN family, an NGO with its head office in Guwahati. RGVN concentrates mainly on generating and enhancing sustainable livelihood opportunities for the rural poor. We as a batch of Seppans can use it as the vehicle to implement some novel ideas. In the following paragraphs, I am listing some of the activities that we may consider taking up.

1. ASSISTANCE IN EDUCATION

In our country, there are many primary schools with a single teacher for three or four classes! In fact, there are schools run by a single unpaid teacher surviving on contributions by the villagers. Such is the appalling teacher – student ratio that the overburdened teacher is not able to do justice to his profession. As a result, it becomes difficult to identify bright students and to give more attention to average or below average students. Governments are often reluctant to acknowledge existence of such gaps in its system which therefore remain unaddressed.

I was wondering whether we could depute someone from that area on stipend basis to take some classes so that the burden on the single teacher is reduced. We could also select a few promising students and tutor them through our volunteers. Here the progress can be measured when they appear for competitive exams for pursuing further education. The most meritorious among them can be made aware of the various scholarships available.

2. COUNSELLING AND UPLIFTMENT MEASURES FOR JOBLESS AND MISGUIDED YOUTH:

There is another segment, concerning jobless youths, school / college dropouts or even graduates, that needs to be addressed and this is for our own safety too. This section of the population is prone to being sucked into anti-social and criminal activities, and into substance abuse. We could identify and engage with such youths who can be motivated into vocational training or trades and make them useful members of society. I have been thinking about a Youth Guidance and Motivation Centre in my organization, which we hope to launch soon. This Centre will receive such youth, counsel them and depending on their needs and aspirations take the next step. These could be like helping them find jobs, sending them for training, connecting them to educational institutions or even arranging finance for the ventures they may wish to pursue.

To speak to these youth, we plan to involve the senior citizens with whom we have been working in the Hello and Millo club, a joint venture of Einstein (Debashish Chowdhury) and Sarbari, his wife, and RGVN.

3. DEVELOPMENT AS A TOURIST VILLAGE:

The village called Bher Bheri about 5-6 kms from the picturesque tourist / picnic spot of Chandubi Lake near Guwahati can be developed as a sustainable tourist village. RGVN has already implemented a solar powered drinking water and construction of sanitary toilets project with Seppan Arup Rajkhowa heading the project team.

4. PROMOTION OF HUSBANDRY IN THE RURAL SECTOR

Animal rearing is a profitable activity for poor households. Poor people do rear pigs, goats, poultry and even a small number of cows or buffalo. These work as their ATMs; whenever they require some money they sell off one or two of them. Poultry provides them a part of their fresh daily nourishment as well as a small income by selling off the surplus eggs. Recently, I advised a micro finance institution -and they had given 700 ducklings to 100 families along with some feed. Duck eggs are nearly twice as expensive as chicken eggs. Through duck breeding, the villagers are able to consume duck eggs and also sell some to buy other household necessities. Encouraged by the success of this project, the organization now wants to undertake some more of such type of activities out of their profit.

Our group could explore such activities as well. We can start in two or three villages. Each village of about hundred families will require about Rs 1.00 lakh for the ducklings and feed and transportation. To make it a sustainable project, we could take back a couple of ducks after they are raised to maturity, sell them and use the proceeds to buy more ducklings for distribution to new households. This practice is followed in the villages and is called the "adhiar" system. We are following such an adhiar system for RGVN's Goats Project in collaboration with Goat Trust, funded by Azim Premji Trust, in Goalpara District of Assam. After the goats breed, a defined number of goat kids are returned to RGVN for redistribution to other needy families making it a profitable and sustainable economic model for rural households

5. PROMOTION OF HOMEGROWN PRODUCE:

Backyard gardening is another initiative we have been promoting in the villages. The villagers can meet their daily needs for vegetables, areca nut, coconut, etc,. Some families even maintain small ponds where they rear fish. In these homestead gardens, they can also grow herbs and medicinal plants. After the produce is aggregated at the village level, it can be sold directly in the market or to larger marketing organizations. Aggregation will be the key to achieving such objectives.

6. PROMOTION OF SENIOR CITIZENS CLUB:

Lastly, some of you can join Einstein and me to work for this club, for which we received some contributions a few years ago. We have promoted the Hello and Millo club where the members come to RGVN on Tuesdays and Fridays for yoga, laughing therapy, singing and dancing, followed by light refreshments. The group currently has about forty members. It's a wonderful community and many of them celebrate their birthdays together. Once in a while, we also take the group out for lunch to some resort or even restaurants. Based on the success of the Hello Millo Club, another such Club was opened by a lady with RGVN support for the initial setup. A third, smaller club is operating in another locality. We are trying to set up a fourth one soon.

Funds are required to pay honorarium to the support staff and rent for the meeting hall. Members pay a token monthly membership fee which just about covers the regular expenses. SEPPA Cares has collected a lot of books to open a Library for the Hello Millo Club members. Since members are expected to walk to the club, we propose to set up more such clubs in different localities.

The success of the club has attracted considerable interest from various organizations—some, like banks, approach the members to promote their investment schemes; some doctors offer genuine support; and research students visit to study the initiative

CONCLUSION

One can see that many things can be done by us if we put our heart, mind and small contributions. Even if a handful of us can jointly take up one or two projects like the ones mentioned above, it will make a great difference. I feel that with the strong team we have at RGVN, it would not be difficult to take up any of the above initiatives. Let us continue our discussions and explore the next step together.

The Hidden History of Shillong's Cherry Blossoms

By Utpal Gohain

How a forgotten 1975 government memo explains today's pink-hued Shillong

A Legacy in Pink

For those of us who studied in Shillong in the 1970s, cherry blossoms were rare—perhaps an odd tree behind a chapel or in someone's private compound. The sweeping pink corridors we now see are the result of a government initiative quietly launched in 1975, nurtured over decades, and finally celebrated through the Cherry Blossom Festival beginning 2016.

Today, when tens of thousands flock to Shillong in November, smartphones raised to capture the pink canopy overhead, they are witnessing the flowering of a simple bureaucratic idea—one that has aged, quite literally, into full bloom.

Every November, Shillong transforms into a painter's dream—soft pink blossoms lining its avenues, a delicate glow settling over Ward's Lake, and crowds gathering to celebrate the now-famous Shillong Cherry Blossom Festival. In less than a decade, the festival has drawn international performers such as Akon, Boney M, Ne-Yo, Diplo and The Script, turning Shillong into India's unlikely stage for global acts. With attendance often crossing 50,000, the festival has firmly placed Meghalaya on the world tourism map.

Cherry blossoms in bloom - Shillong today

Shillong's cherry blossoms are more than just a tourist attraction. They are a testament to vision, patience, and the long arc of public service. And they carry with them the legacy of officers who believed that beauty, too, can be part of governance.

And yet, for many who grew up in Shillong in the 1960s and 70s, a quiet question lingers: Where were these cherry blossoms when we were children?

That was the mystery that confronted me when I joined the Facebook group Reminiscences of Shillong nearly ten years ago. Members frequently posted stunning photos of cherry trees in full bloom—captioned "Pink Showers," "Magic in the Air," and "Shillong in November." The images were breathtaking. But they also puzzled many of us who had left Shillong decades earlier. None of us remembered seeing such a profusion of cherry blossoms during our school years. Were we simply unobservant children? Or had memory played a trick on us?

The truth arrived, quite unexpectedly, in December 2020.

A Letter from the Past

A member of Tripura's erstwhile royal family uploaded an old government letter dated 10 December 1975. The sender: N.K. Rustomji, then Chief Secretary of Meghalaya. The recipient: B.N. Das, Indian Forest Service, who was serving as the Conservator of Forests. The subject of the letter was disarmingly simple: a proposal for a "massive programme for planting cherry trees alongside our roads during the planting season."

The 1975 Government Letter from N.K. Rustomji proposing the cherry tree plantation program

Suddenly, everything made sense.

Rustomji noted that the Governor had shown interest in the idea. Copies of the letter were marked to Shillong Municipality CEO A.H. Scott and Town & Country Planning Secretary F. Kharkongor. What followed was a coordinated tree-plantation drive across Shillong—quiet, unadvertised, and entirely utilitarian in tone. Yet it laid the foundation for the vibrant cherry blossom landscape we see today.

For many long-time Shillongites, the discovery was nothing short of a revelation. The cherry blossoms so proudly associated with today's Shillong had simply not been part of their childhood—and for a very good reason: they did not exist back then.

When I posted the 1975 government letter in the Reminiscences of Shillong Facebook group, a wave of nostalgia and astonishment swept through the comments section. Memories long buried resurfaced, now illuminated by a newfound clarity.

"That's why I always wondered how we never used to see them in our youth." — Ranel Das, Guwahati

"I only remember plum and peach trees back then." — Nandita Chalam, Mumbai

"Left Shillong in 1976—no wonder I missed them." — Murari Saikia, Tezpur

For decades, these questions lingered quietly in the background. The letter had finally put those doubts to rest.

The Men Behind the Blossoms

The letter also opened a window into a remarkable chapter of Shillong's administrative history.

N.K. Rustomji - Meghalaya's first Chief Secretary

Nari Rustomji, among the last of the distinguished ICS officers, served as Political Officer in Bhutan and Sikkim before becoming Meghalaya's first Chief Secretary in 1972. Upright and fiercely committed to public service, he was known to walk across Shillong with his towering Alsatians, noting down issues for various departments.

One former Shillong resident, Ruma Phukan, shared a charming vignette:

Rustomji noticed his dogs repeatedly barking at a pine tree near the telegraph office. Concerned, he alerted the Forest Department. When they dug around the roots, they found anthills eating into the tree. Remedial action saved it.

Such anecdotes reveal a public servant who paid attention—to trees, to people, to the city he served.

Bhupendra Nath Das, the forest officer who received the letter, went on to become Assam's Chief Conservator of Forests, and was respected for his integrity and expertise. And Aubrey Herbert Scott Lyngdoh, the Shillong Municipality CEO copied on the memo, later became Meghalaya's Finance Minister. He also happened to be the father of Neil Nongkynrih, founder of the iconic Shillong Chamber Choir.

Cherry Blossom Festival at Ward's Lake, Shillong

These were the men who, knowingly or unknowingly, shaped the Shillong of today—one cherry sapling at a time.

Books That We Used to Read

By Utpal Gohain

Before the advent of television and computers in our everyday lives, books and films were our windows to the world. Watching a movie was an occasional treat, so books were our primary source of knowledge and entertainment. Radio was mostly for news and popular songs, and even that was restricted to a few hours of broadcast time from All India Radio and Radio Ceylon. For the rest of the day, it was comics and books of various genres and catering to all age groups —that delighted us.

We owe much of our reading habits to the School Library, which had an abundance of fiction and non-fiction books, as well as magazines like National Geographic, Life and Time. Again, the State Central Library had an impressive collection of books, and accessed by many day scholars. Of course, Modern Book Depot in Police Bazar was amply stocked with books of all genres, and was a popular haunt for the book lovers among us, where we made our purchases with our pocket money.

I was born much after the Second World War, yet I knew a great deal about it even before high school. Names like Dunkirk and Normandy, the shapes of helmets worn by soldiers on different sides, and dramatic foreign exclamations such as "Gott in Himmel!", "Achtung!" "Schweinehund!" and "Banzai!", were all familiar long before any formal history lesson. We learnt these from the illustrated Commando comics. We knew that the Spitfire was the Royal Air Force's main fighter plane and the Messerschmitt Me 109 was its Luftwaffe counterpart. The RAF flew Mosquito fighter-bombers and Lancaster bombers, while the Luftwaffe deployed Stuka dive bombers. We even nicknamed two of our venerable Irish Christian Brothers "German General" and "SS Colonel" because they fit our imagined versions of how German officers should look.

Apart from war comics, we devoured cowboy adventures of the Wild West—heroes like Pecos Bill and Calamity Jane, and American Indian tribes such as the Sioux, Pawnees, Navajos and Apache became familiar names.

I had hardly read Shakespeare, Victor Hugo, Dickens, Jules Verne or Mark Twain—but I knew all their stories, thanks to Classics Illustrated comic book series. In sixty or seventy pages of vivid panels, the world's great literature unfolded before us.

Then came characters who stayed with us for years: The Phantom (The Ghost Who Walks), Tarzan, Korak (son of Tarzan, Mandrake the Magician, Flash Gordon and the superheroes of DC Comics—Superman, Batman and Spiderman. Archie, Betty, Veronica and Jughead gave us our first glimpse into American school life and fast-food culture.

By middle school, we graduated to "real" books—The Famous Five, Secret Seven, and the Mystery series by Enid Blyton, The Hardy Boys by Franklin W. Dixon, Biggles by Captain W.E. Johns, Billy Bunter by Frank Richards and William by Richmal Crompton. Boys in British-style schools everywhere read these—and we at St. Edmund's were no exception.

My father was a passionate reader of detective and spy fiction, and his bookshelf held some treasures: Ian Fleming's James Bond novels like From Russia With Love, Moonraker, Casino Royale and The Man with the Golden Gun, as well as Perry Mason mysteries by Erle Stanley Gardner. Compared to the thrilling pace of the Sean Connery 007 films, these books felt slower—but they were compelling.

My first James Hadley Chase novel—There's a Hippie on the Highway—hooked me instantly. Chase was prolific, and for a while, his books were everywhere. The titles alone seemed to leap off the shelves: The Vulture Is a Patient Bird, You're Nothing Without Money, An Ace Up My Sleeve. The covers were bold—sometimes scandalous to young eyes. Much later, I was shocked to learn that Chase, despite writing so vividly about American crime, had never been to the United States. His gangsters, cities and femme fatales were pure imagination.

Chase led us toward "serious" thrillers — Alistair MacLean with Where Eagles Dare, The Guns of Navarone, Ice Station Zebra, Fear is the Key and Puppet on a Chain; Frederick Forsyth's The Day of the Jackal and The Odessa File; Desmond Bagley's High Citadel, Running Blind and Ken Follett's Eye of the Needle.

Other famed novelists of our times were Arthur Hailey (Hotel, Airport), A J Cronin (The Stars Look Down, The Citadel) and historical fiction writer, Leon Uris (Exodus, Armageddon, Battle Cry).

Then there were the "smut" and pulp fiction series on Nick Carter and Ted Mark. Of course, topping this genre of adult reading bordering on the prurient, was Harold Robbins (Betsy, Carpet Baggers, 79 Park Avenue, Dream Merchants. This category of fiction was read surreptitiously, as it was frowned upon by the Christian Brothers. There were known instances of bag checks and confiscation of such prized books by a class teacher!

Crime fiction would be incomplete without Agatha Christie and her unforgettable detectives—Hercule Poirot and Miss Marple. And, of course, Sir Arthur Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes, whose Hound of the Baskervilles and The Speckled Band remain masterpieces.

Then there were books we were supposed to read—assigned by our teachers: Future Shock by Alvin Toffler, I'm OK, You're OK by Thomas Anthony Harris, Jonathan Livingston Seagull by Richard Bach and Run Baby Run by Nicky Cruz. Sunil Gavaskar's autobiography Sunny Days became a bestseller in schoolboy circles.

The Western genre was another favourite. We followed Sudden—James Green, the legendary gunslinger created by Oliver Strange and later continued by Frederick H. Christian. Another well-read writer in this genre was Loius L'Amour (Shalako, Catlow, Silver Canyon). Morgan Kane, created by Kjell Hallbing under the pseudonym Louis Masterson, and Jubal Cade (published under the shared pseudonym Charles R. Pike) also became companions during quieter afternoons.

And then—P.G. Wodehouse. My first book was The Empress of Blandings. The title led me to expect a grand lady—but instead I found a delightfully pampered pig. After that, I was a convert. Even today, over fifty years later, some of my classmates still slip into that cheerful, stiff-upper-lip, old-chap sort of language in messages, much to the bewilderment of outsiders.

I could write pages more. This list could stretch into the thousands of words. But perhaps it is enough to say that if a Gen Z child ever asked what we did for fun in our schooldays and we replied, "We read books," they may stare at us in disbelief!

It is sad that many of us no longer find the time or inclination to read for leisure—a pastime that once brought us immense joy.

Shillong Pop & Rock Scene of the 60s and 70s

By MKDG

Shillong has, since the 60s, been referred to as the Rock Capital of India due to its vibrant and strong rock music culture and talented native musicians: such a status is the outcome of early Western musical exposure and a concentrated youth culture. Concerts took place at regular intervals in various venues like the State Central Library, School and College fetes, Evening Club, Pinewood Hotel, etc. Most of the bands did gigs in Calcutta's Park Street in the 60s and 70s, thereby earning wider acclaim and recognition beyond the precincts of the Northeast region. This page takes you through a journey depicting in short, the prominent pop / rock bands of our times...

Lou Majaw

If there is one living rock legend in India, it surely is Lou Majaw. From the 1960s, he has been a trailblazer in various genres of music, be it folk, hard rock, blues, having been in various bands from the early years with Dynamite Boys, then Vanguards, Supersound Factory, Blood and Thunder and the Great Society in the mid 70s. During our school years, we have mostly known him as the front man of Blood and Thunder. Known as the Shillong Dylan, he has been organising an annual Bob Dylan Tribute concert on 24th May, Dylan's birthday, since 1972. Still going strong at 78 years, he is "forever young", and is also famed for his idiosyncratic habit of wearing mismatched socks!

Eddie Rynjah

Another great Shillong rock icon of our times was Eddie Rynjah. He started off with the Vaudevilles in the 60s, and later on, with the Supersound Factory. He was associated with many other bands after our school days, like New Era, Blue Sky and Powerhouse, but he was largely a solo artiste with back up musicians. His signature baritone golden voice was well known, and he was versatile in various genres of music like folk, blues, hard rock. Unfortunately, he passed away at a relatively young age of 49 years in 1997.

The Fentones

The band that catapulted Shillong into national recognition were the Fentones, when they were the first band from the Northeast to win, against stiff competition, the Simla Beat Contest in 1971. They were spearheaded by Sherlock Giri on lead guitar and Roland as lead vocalist. The Fentones not only did covers, but also composed original songs. They came into the scene in the 60s, and continued till the late 90s. I had the fortune to see them and Blood and Thunder perform live at Stonyland Fete in the late 60s.

The Vanguards

The Vanguards were perhaps the first famous pop/rock band from Shillong, bursting onto the scene in the mid sixties. Not only popular in Shillong, they made their mark in Calcutta's Park Street nightspots too (read Trincas), where almost all the Shillong bands played at some point of time.

The Vaudevilles

The Vaudevilles were a pioneering pop/rock band formed in 1962 by the Rynjah brothers (Peter, Jimmy and David). They too performed to rousing reception at Calcutta's Trincas in the late sixties. For three years in a row (1975, 1976 and 1977) they won the Meghalaya Festival Beat Contest. They disbanded in 1986, after an adventurous musical journey of 24 years.

Fads and Pastimes During Our School Years

By MKDG

Playing Marbles: Playing marbles was one of the most popular pastimes in our junior classes. There were two formats of the game: reals and funnings. Reals was when the victor could, for good, claim the marbles he dislodged outside the ring where the marbles were placed by the players, and funnings was a "friendly" game, and you retained ownership of the marbles after the game was over. The "shooter" marbles were of two types: hard clay taws white in colour, and the more effective "iondoms" which were steel balls from automotive bearings. The main arena for playing marbles was by the side of the Junior Dorm and the Stone Building.

The real yo-yos were a craze when we were in Class VI-VII, and there was a Yo-yo King among us, Kiran Bhuyan, our senior. If you got hold of old and discarded typewriter spools, they also served the purpose of a Yo-yo!

Dinky Toy Cars: These are dinky toy cars (as also trucks, busses) that most of us had during our junior years. Boarders used to "run" them down the sloping drain (as in the picture) between First Field and the adjoining lower field.

Tops: Tops were a rage in our junior years. They came in assorted shapes and sizes. Your skill as a top spinner was measured by the time the top could spin smoothly. As homes in Shillong then had wooden planked floors, which was not conducive to top spinning, dayskies spun their tops in the cemented areas outside the building. As for the boarders, favourite arenas were the summer house and the portico below the then façade of the Main Building.

Pea Shooters: Pea shooters caught our fancy in our middle years (Classes V, VI). The barrel was a thin bamboo stem, and the pellets were green coloured seeds from a wild bush. Hopscotch: Hopscotch was a popular pastime for all of us; being a unisex game, dayskies' sibling sisters / neighbourhood boys and girls could join in it for a bit of fun. For boarders, the cemented areas were the locales for this game.

Classroom Games: Then there were "time passes" (this expression, which is exclusively Indian English, came into existence only in the early eighties) that were popular in our senior years, which we played during classroom breaks, or in general, leisure periods at any time: 1) Hangman 2) Battleships and Destroyers 3) Dots and boxes 4) Noughts and Crosses

Paper Crafts: In our junior years, we made good use of paper from old exercise books to make paper boats (to ply them on small bath tubs / puddles in school or near our homes) and paper rockets which were flown around in the classrooms or our homes.

Fashions and Styles During Our School Years

By MKDG

Shillong in the 1960s and 70s: A Style Story of Its Own

Shillong — the capital of the erstwhile undivided state of Assam until 1972, and thereafter of the newly created state of Meghalaya — was, despite its geographical remoteness, always a remarkably cosmopolitan town. Throughout much of the twentieth century, the city absorbed and reflected trends from the style capitals of India — Bombay (now Mumbai) and Calcutta (now Kolkata) — as well as influences from the West. During our school years, this harmonious blending of local, national and international fashion was distinctly visible in the corridors, classrooms and common spaces we inhabited.

The mid-1950s and 1960s, saw the West undergoing sweeping cultural changes brought about by anti-establishment expressions which were influenced by geo-political events such as the Vietnam war, the draft and anti-war movements in the US. Rock and roll, led by the Beatles and the phenomenon of Beatlemania, the Rolling Stones, Elvis Presley and others, electrified global youth and spawned counterculture movements. These movements had seminal impact on the way people looked and dressed and also significantly altered the way the generation communicated. The influence was so deep that youth, even in distant pockets of the world, wanted to be part of the 'in' crowd.

India was not insulated from these winds of change — and neither was Shillong. Many subtle reflections of this broader cultural churn found their way into our campus life. What follows is a nostalgic look at some of the trends that coloured those memorable school years.

Hairstyles

Longish hair slowly became fashionable in the early 1970s, but the Irish Christian Brothers ensured that it never crossed "acceptable" limits. Morning Assembly often doubled as an inspection point, where both attire and hair length were scrutinised.

Growing sidelocks became a fad. A handful of bold students even experimented with peroxide-tinted streaks — among them, Prithviraj Poon of our Section A class. Others like Shyam Singhania and senior Naseer Ahmed also sported the look.

Meanwhile, Shillong's legendary musicians — Lou Majaw, Eddie Rynjah and others — embraced true rock-star style with their flowing waist-length hair from the late 1960s.

Trousers

The 1960s belonged to drainpipe trousers, slim and sharply tapered. At the turn of the decade, bell bottoms made their debut. By 1972, parallels found favour and by the mid-1970s, flares — dramatic versions of bell bottoms with a striking contrast between thigh and hem — became the height of fashion.

Whether these styles at school were gracefully accepted gracefully by the Brothers or were simply tolerated is an unanswered question. But the point is nobody was ever punished for flaunting the latest styles as long as they were of the school uniform white drill or grey flannels.

Shirts, Pullovers and Blazers

Late 1960s casual wear saw the rising popularity of colourful bush shirts and round-collar shirts. By the mid-1970s, the flamboyant dog-ear collar entered the scene, often inspired by Bollywood heroes of the time. Turtleneck pullovers also gained traction and became a favourite among students.

While the official uniform required single-breasted blazers, some stylish seniors in the 1970s boldly sported double-breasted versions.

Belts

The school belt — a sturdy canvas with the classic tri-colours of gold, maroon and green — was instantly recognisable and universally worn.

For casual wear, however, the 1970s ushered in a craze for wide 5-cm leather belts, resulting in trouser loops being specially tailored to accommodate them.

Shoes & Boots

In tandem with the above mentioned sartorial trends, some of the more stylish boys in our school wore black Beatle boots to school from the late 60s, but this was only a miniscule minority and limited to use by dayscholars: this deviation from the standard laced shoes was apparently overlooked by the school authorities. High heeled platform shoes also started catching on from the early seventies.

Looking back, the fashions of our school years were much more than temporary styles — they were subtle markers of identity, confidence and belonging. In a disciplined environment guided by the Irish Christian Brothers, we still found small ways to express the rapidly changing world around us.

Every hairstyle experiment, every new trouser cut, every colourful shirt became a symbol of youthful curiosity and our connection to larger cultural currents. They reflected the songs we listened to, the films we admired, and the shifting social tides sweeping across India and the world.

Today, these memories form a vivid collage: drainpipes and bell bottoms, bush shirts and turtlenecks, peroxide streaks, double-breasted blazers — each fashion echoing a friendship, a moment, or a chapter of our growing-up years. Though decades have passed, the camaraderie and the spirit behind those styles remain as alive as ever.

St Edmund's School: For most of us, we were familiar with the old Main Building of St Edmund's School as seen in the B&W photo. It was only during our last couple of years, the new RCC building came up, and has remained that way more or less since the past five decades.

Pinewood Hotel: Pinewood Hotel was Shillong's pre-eminent hotel in the twentieth century. It was built in 1898 by a Swiss couple. During our school years, this British era heritage property was under private ownership until, with the formation of the state of Meghalaya, the newly constituted Meghalaya Tourism Development Corporation took over management and ownership in 1982, initially called "Hotel Pinewood Ashok". In recent years, the Hotel has added an annex modern Convention Centre.

Shillong Club: Shillong Club was established in 1878 which had become the headquarters of the Chief Commissionership Assam. Till Independence, this was an exclusive "Whites Only" club. After 1947, due to the huge exodus of Europeans from India, the Club admitted Indians from the administrative elite, armed forces and the judiciary. A huge fire broke out in 1962, largely destroying the original structures. A new structure was constructed thereafter, which stands to this day.

Legislative Assembly Building: The old Legislative Assembly Building was a familiar site to all of us during our school years, being located at Police Bazar Point. The foundation stone of this heritage Gothic style building was laid in 1921 and served as composite Assam Assembly from 1937 till 1973, when in 1974, it became the sole Assembly House of the newly formed state of Meghalaya. Unfortunately, a devastating fire gutted the entire structure in 2001, and in its place, the new administrative block of Meghalaya Legislative Assembly was constructed.

State Central Library: The State Central Library of Shillong was another iconic landmark during our times. Constructed in the early 50s by the then Assam Government, it was the pre-eminent library-cum cultural centre of undivided Assam, and from January 1974, it became under the custody of the newly formed Meghalaya Government. Not only was the library famed for its immense and veritable collection of books (fiction and non-fiction), magazines and journals catering to all ages (though mostly in English, but also had Hindi, Bengali, Assamese, Khasi and Garo publications), its auditorium was the venue of cultural and entertainment functions, which live music concerts (Western and Indian), dance programmes, magic shows (both PC Sorcar Sr and Jr graced this venue), the historic "moon rock" display, and many more.

Polo Grounds: The Polo Grounds, another historical landmark of Shillong, was established by the British in the early 1890s, primarily for the game of polo and horse racing. Since 1901, these grounds have been managed by the Shillong Recreation Ground Trust. During our times, polo and horse racing had petered out, and the main events that took place were football, cricket and archery (teer), which continue to be the mainstay sports to this day in these Grounds. In 1972, the Main Stadium in the Grounds, Jawaharlal Nehru Stadium, was inaugurated. Today, the Grounds have retained its pre eminence not only as the premier sporting venue in Shillong, but also for major cultural and musical events: the Scorpions and Bryan Adams played to packed crowds in these Grounds.

Modern Book Depot (MBD): Modern Book Depot in Police Bazar was a book lover's paradise during our times: it had an amazing collection of fiction and non-fiction books (catering to all ages) and of course comics. It also had a section for records (45s and LPs). For the dayskie, it was a treat when the old folks took one to the shop. For the Boarders upwards from Class VI, it was a favourite haunt during Saturday afternoon walks from School to PB. Unfortunately, MBD was gutted to ashes in a devastating fire in 1988.

Kelvin Cinema: Another iconic entertainment landmark in Shillong was Kelvin Cinema in Police Bazar, the first talkies in Shillong established in 1926, which screened top class English and Bengali movies. Unfortunately, along with the Modern Book Depot, this hall was gutted by fire in 1988. A Kelvin Cinema Festival was first held in 2019, and then in 2023, in honour and memory of this iconic cinema hall.

Shillong City Buses: One can never forget the Shillong City buses in which we travelled frequently on the main bus routes, sometimes packed like sardines and hanging from the footboards (notice the position of the exhaust pipe!). Earlier times, we had Hindustan Bedford, then Tata Mercedes Benz and then the homegrown Tata models with "protruding engine" models among them. One thing that has not changed over the decades is the two tone colour blue/bluish green and yellow of these buses.

Chapala Book Stall: Chapala Book Stall on Jail Road, Police Bazar, was the most popular book store in Shillong for school and college text books, and for general readers, it had a wide range of books in English and vernacular languages, competitive exam guides and general literature. Established in 1936, as one of Shillong's oldest book shops, the building, constructed in 1901, where the book stall was housed during our times, has been demolished since and a new development complex is coming up on the site. In July 2020, Chapala Book Stall shifted to its new premises in the Brahmo Samaj Complex on Keating Road.

Down Shop: Down Shop, just outside the School Gates, was a seedy (then) den whose regular clientele were the senior boys especially dayskies, and boarders (who would hook out) for their dose of kwai and fags. The establishment appears to have prospered over the years, selling sundry items over and above the signature kwai and fags.
